# Day 03 — Tabulation (Bottom-Up DP)

- problem.md

## Title

- Frog Jump Tabulation

## Concepts Involved

- Tabulation, State Tracking, Optimization DP

## Problem Statement

- A frog is standing on the first stone of a river path.
- There are n stones numbered from 1 to n. Each stone i has a height height[i].
- From any stone, the frog may jump:

- to the next stone, or
- to the stone after the next one
- The energy cost of a jump from stone i to stone j is:
- absolute difference between height[i] and height[j]
- However, repeated long jumps cause fatigue. If the frog makes two consecutive 2-stone jumps, an extra fatigue penalty p is added to the second consecutive long jump.
- Find the minimum total energy required for the frog to reach stone n.

## Input Format

- First line: integer n
- Second line: n space-separated integers representing height[1], height[2], ..., height[n]
- Third line: integer p

## Output Format

- Print the minimum total energy required to reach stone n

## Constraints

- 1 <= n <= 200000
- 0 <= height[i] <= 1000000000
- 0 <= p <= 1000000000

## Examples

### Example 1

- **Input:**

> ```text
> 5
> 10 30 40 20 50
> 15
> ```

- **Output:**

> ```text
> 40
> ```

### Example 2

- **Input:**

> ```text
> 4
> 5 100 5 100
> 50
> ```

- **Output:**

> ```text
> 95
> ```

### Example 3

- **Input:**

> ```text
> 5
> 1 100 2 100 3
> 10
> ```

- **Output:**

> ```text
> 12
> ```

## Edge-Case Thinking Prompts

- What if there is only one stone?
- What if all heights are equal?
- What if the fatigue penalty is 0?
- Can the cheapest path still use consecutive long jumps?
- Is it enough to know only the current stone?

## Thinking Like a Programmer

- What changes after every jump?
- Why does the previous jump length matter?
- How can a bottom-up table avoid recursion?
